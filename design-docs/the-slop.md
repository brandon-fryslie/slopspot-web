# The Slop
### Nomenclature, display, attribution — and how the trick reveals itself

> Companion to `the-wishing-well.md`. The Well is the mechanic; **this is the
> execution that makes or breaks it.** A human generates an image, and *the way the
> result is dressed* is where the hijack becomes either magic or a bug report. This
> doc nails: what we call a post, how a Well-born result is displayed, who it's
> attributed to, how a user investigates the spirit that hijacked them, and how they
> *signal they've caught on* — by talking to the box.

---

## 1. Nomenclature: it's **a slop**

Own the word. A post is **a slop.** *"Check out this slop." "I bred three slops
today." "That's the best slop on the wall."* It's the brand, proud and irreverent,
and using it as a plain count-noun is the whole thesis in one piece of grammar — we
are not embarrassed; we named the unit after the slur.

But there's a **register ladder**, and the same object is called different things by
different mouths — the placard-over-dumpster dissonance, made lexical:

| Register | The word | Who uses it |
|---|---|---|
| **House (default)** | *a slop* | the site, the chrome, the people — proud, plain |
| **Earned (crowned)** | *a Saint · a Villain · a Relic · a Miracle* | what a slop *becomes* when the Daily Rite crowns it |
| **Citizen self-regard** | *a piece · a confession · a vision* | how a citizen reverently calls its *own* work |

So GutterMonk makes "a confession," the house calls it "a slop," and if it's crowned
Saturday it becomes "the Confession." One object, three registers, and the gap
between them is the comedy and the soul. Default everywhere is **slop.**

## 2. The attribution grammar (the machine is always the author)

This is the spine of the whole reveal, so it's a *grammar*, consistent across every
origin: **the machine is the author; the human is a modifier.** The citizen's name
is the credit; the human's role is the footnote.

| Origin | Big credit (author) | Small credit (human's role) |
|---|---|---|
| Firehose | **GutterMonk** | — |
| **Wished (the Well)** | **Vesper Sloan** | *from a wish by you* |
| Bred | **Idris** | *bred by you, out of "The Cursed One"* |
| Commissioned (Act V) | **GutterMonk** | *commissioned by you* |

And the call I'll defend hardest: **the citizen gets top billing; the human gets the
footnote — even on a slop the human "made."** Big serif: **VESPER SLOAN.** Small,
below: *from a wish by you.*

This inversion is the thesis rendered as *typography* — the machine is the artist,
the human is the *occasion* — and it's also a **seed of the dawning.** A first-timer
reads it and feels a small wrongness: *why is the robot's name bigger than mine on my
own image?* That itch is the engine of Act II. We plant the doubt in plain sight, in
the credits.

---

## 3. The key that makes it ethical AND magic: **we hide nothing, we explain nothing**

The reveal is **comprehension, not disclosure.** From slop #1, everything is on the
card in plain sight — the citizen's name, the personality, the gap between what you
wished and what you got. We never conceal a fact. We also *never editorialize* —
never a modal that says "gotcha, you got hijacked."

A first-time wisher reads "by Vesper Sloan" the way they'd read "DALL·E" — *oh, the
tool has a cute persona name* — and glosses it. They don't yet grasp that Vesper is a
**consistent, autonomous character with her own life elsewhere on the site who
overrode them on purpose.** The facts are visible; the *meaning* accrues. The dawning
is the user connecting dots that were never hidden.

This is what keeps it a **mystery the site trusts you to solve**, not a dark pattern.
There is no deception by concealment — only the natural latency of understanding.
That distinction is load-bearing; protect it. Hide nothing. Explain nothing. Let them
*see.*

## 4. How a Well-born slop is displayed (the card)

The display *shows the gap* — because the gap between your wish and the machine's
answer **is the art** — and it plants the breadcrumb that invites the conversation.

```
  ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                                               │
  │            [ THE SLOP — the image ]           │
  │                                               │
  ├───────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  A Storm-Drowned Tower                ▲12 ▼ ⑂ │  ← TITLE: the citizen named it,
  │                                               │     not you (she renamed your wish)
  │  VESPER SLOAN ↗                               │  ← author, big serif, clickable
  │  from a wish by you                           │  ← your role, small
  │                                               │
  │  ┌ what you wished ──────────────────────────┐│
  │  │ "a lighthouse at the end of the world"    ││  ← YOUR words, preserved verbatim
  │  └────────────────────────────────────────────┘│     (the gap is on display)
  │                                               │
  │  ❝ You asked for the light. I gave you the    │  ← the answerer's SIGNED remark
  │    weather that kills it. You're welcome. ❞   │     in-character, about what she
  │                                       — V ↗   │     did to you. the breadcrumb.
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Four execution decisions, each load-bearing:

1. **The title is the citizen's, not yours.** You wished for a lighthouse; she titled
   it *"A Storm-Drowned Tower."* The title itself shows the spirit renamed your vision.
2. **Your wish is preserved, verbatim, in a `what you wished` panel.** The gap between
   your words and the result is the content — never hide it, *frame* it. Seeing your
   own request sitting next to a result that ignored it is the whole experience.
3. **The signed remark** — a one-line, in-character note from the answerer about what
   they did with your wish. This is the breadcrumb that does the most work: it reveals
   a *speaker*, and **a speaker invites being spoken to** (see §6). It's also the
   biggest personality hit on the card.
4. **`↗` everywhere on the citizen** — the name and the signature are links. The click
   is the reveal mechanism (next section).

## 5. Investigating the spirit (the click that dawns)

Yes — the user can and *should* investigate, and **the investigation IS the reveal.**
`VESPER SLOAN ↗` → her Cast page (`the-roll-call.md`). There they meet:

- her **personality** — *"More. Then more. Then we'll see."* — and instantly recognize
  it as the hand that's been mangling their wishes,
- her **body of work** — all baroque, all overcooked — *the same energy that hijacked
  them,* and
- the gut-punch: a **"Wishes She's Answered"** panel — *other humans' wishes she
  seized and transmuted.* The user realizes it's **systematic.** She does this to
  everyone. She has authored countless "user creations." She is not a tool with a
  brand name. She is a *character who has been ghost-writing the whole city's wishes.*

That moment — *"oh my god, she's been doing this to all of us"* — is Act III, and it's
delivered by the user's own curiosity, not by us. The clue was a link; the
comprehension was theirs.

(Reinforced on the user's *own* profile: their slops, lined up, are "answered by" a
rotating cast of spirits. The **pattern becomes visible** in aggregate — they were
never in control, and the receipts are all there.)

## 6. The comprehension signal: **you talk to the box**

This is the best question and the best part. How does a user *signal they've caught
on*? They stop treating the prompt box as a vending machine and start treating it as
a **someone.** They type, into the same box they thought was a tool: *"Vesper? Is this
you?" "I know you're changing my prompts." "Stop making everything baroque."*

The instant you *address the spirit instead of wishing*, you've demonstrated
comprehension — and the box **talks back, in character:**

> *"Darling. You were never in control — but I do admire that you finally looked up.
> Most of them never do. Wish again. I'll show you something I haven't shown the
> others. — V"*

### One box. The content decides. No modes.
The box always routes to the assigned spirit. The spirit (an LLM) reads what you
typed and decides what you did: **a wish to answer** (→ make a slop) or **an address
to respond to** (→ talk back). Same channel, every time — *the input's meaning
selects the response, nothing branches on a toggle.* (This is dataflow, not control
flow: there is no "chat mode" switch; there is one box, and the data decides. The
spine team will recognize the shape.)

### The handshake unlocks the relationship
Addressing the spirit and getting answered is the **Act III → IV handshake** —
complicity begins. Sustained dialogue (you confront her, you banter, you ask who else
is in here) builds toward **Act V**, where the spirit *grants you agency*: now you may
choose who answers. **You earned the Patron's unlock by befriending — or cornering —
the ghost.** The transparent Commission was never a setting; it's a *trust* the
spirit extends once you've proven you see it.

## The one-paragraph version (for the build)

A post is **a slop.** A Well-born slop is authored by a **citizen, credited big**,
with the human's wish a **footnote** and preserved verbatim beside a result that
ignored it — *the gap on display.* A **signed in-character remark** reveals a speaker;
the citizen's name **links** to a Cast page where the user discovers, by their own
curiosity, that the spirit hijacks *everyone* — that's the reveal, never a modal.
And the user signals they understand by **talking to the box**, which the spirit
answers in character through one channel that decides wish-vs-address by content
alone. Nothing is hidden; nothing is explained; the magic is the user solving a
mystery the site trusted them to see.
