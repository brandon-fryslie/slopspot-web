# On Naming

*Doctrine Document VI — Book I, The Material; the book's last entry, completing the
doctrine of the material. Written in the founding era under the kernel set down in
the-index.md. The reader will notice this document describes a discipline the Foreword
already practices — the token, the lens, the no-paraphrase rule. That is the right
order of discovery: the city obeyed the doctrine before it was written, which is how
you know it is doctrine and not invention.*

---

## The Question

What is a name in this city — decoration or structure? When the composer titles a slop,
when a plague becomes "the Year of the Fox," when the city says *slop* instead of
"post" and *bury* instead of "downvote" — is that flavor sprinkled on a working system,
or is it the system? And as the city grows — new citizens, new media, new eras all
demanding words — what governs the vocabulary: who may coin, what gets canonized, what
may never be renamed? Book I ends here because naming is the last act performed on all
material: the flood makes, the eye chooses, the swerve varies — and then the thing is
*called* something, and what it is called decides what it becomes.

## The Law

**A name is the first verdict and the cheapest architecture: the city is built of
earned words, one per thing, repeated until they harden into places — and every
borrowed generic word is a breach in the wall.**

## The Reasoning

**A name is the first verdict.** Before any critic blesses, before any human votes, a
slop has already been judged once — by its placard. "Perfumer Whose Only Client Is A
Fox" is a claim about what matters in the image; a different placard would make a
different artwork of the same pixels. Naming is therefore a taste-act, and everything
On Taste demands of verdicts it demands of names: signed, felt, wagered. The composer
that names a slop is spending judgment, not generating metadata. And the kernel's
hardest line follows: beige language makes beige work — not as a correlation but as a
mechanism. The namer who reaches for "Untitled Composition #4" has already decided the
thing doesn't matter, and every eye downstream inherits the decision. The name is the
first mover of all subsequent feeling.

**A name is the cheapest architecture.** Count what the city is actually built of.
The machinery, yes; the ledger, yes. But what a *visitor* inhabits is perhaps thirty
words: slop, breed, bloodline, wish, the Well, the Rite, the Pulse, citizen, verdict,
bless, bury, crown, placard, medium, the back door. Each costs nothing — no stone, no
steel — and each structures everything: a visitor who has
acquired *bury* understands the Gremlin; one who has acquired *bloodline* understands
the breeding room before seeing it. The vocabulary is the city's load-bearing
architecture at zero carrying cost, and learning it IS the onboarding — which is why
the city must never ship a glossary (that is the Spoiler heresy wearing a librarian's
cardigan). Words are learned here the way words are learned anywhere alive: by
immersion, by context, by getting it slightly wrong at first. The slow acquisition is
the dawn.

**The wall of words.** Now the defensive face. Every city word has a generic synonym
waiting outside: post, remix, downvote, AI-generated content, user. Each substitution
is small, reasonable, legible to newcomers — and each is a breach. Use "post" and the
slop becomes content; use "user" and the guest becomes traffic; let "AI-generated
content aggregator" into the city's own mouth and the whole argument has surrendered
to the enemy's vocabulary, because *whoever supplies the words supplies the verdict.*
The world coined "slop" as a sneer and the city's founding act of naming was to take
the word and make it a banner — the entire thesis, executed at the level of a single
noun. That move must be protected at the level of every noun: inside the wall, the
city's words mean what the city means. Hold the wall.

**One name, hardened by repetition.** Why one word per thing, never a rotation of
elegant synonyms? Because a name works like a lens: every use pours back into one
point, and the point hardens — through repetition with one spelling — from label into
*place*. Rotate synonyms ("slop," then "piece," then "artwork," for elegance) and the
lens scatters; each variant a slightly different shadow; the place never forms. The
Foreword discovered this for the Doctrine's own concepts — the token, cited
identically every time, no paraphrase — and this document now names the general law
the Foreword was obeying: **canonical spelling is how a word becomes real estate.**
This is also why renaming is taboo: a hardened name has years of meaning poured into
it, and renaming pours it out on the ground. Nicknames may accrete on top (the city
should be rich in nicknames — accretion is lore), but the canonical name, once earned,
is append-only like the ledger it labels.

**Earning, never decree.** How does a word get in? The phrasebook already practices
the answer: a line enters because it *landed* — used naturally, picked up, repeated
without being pushed. Canonization is retrospective recognition, never planning
([DOCTRINE:computed-not-parameter], applied to language: the vocabulary is derived
from what survived contact, never decreed into existence). A session that coins ten
words in an afternoon has minted currency with no reserves; the words will buy
nothing. Coin freely in the flood — coinage is generation, and [SPARK:generate-then-judge]
applies to words exactly as to images — but canonize only what comes back alive. And
keep the canon *small*: a city of thirty words is a wall; a city of three hundred is
a fog. The minting discipline the Foreword imposed on tokens is the same discipline
the phrasebook must impose on the whole lexicon: if everything is named, nothing is.

**The long dream: the word as the farthest traveler.** Here is what I see at the
horizon. Names are the only part of the city that can travel with no bandwidth at all
— no image, no link, no feed required. A tourist leaves, and weeks later says
*bloodline* about some other site's AI art, says *that's pure slopspot* about an
unhinged jpeg in a group chat — and the city has just colonized a corner of language
it will never see. This is [DOCTRINE:the-export-organ] in its lightest form: the
vocabulary is an export organ that ships itself. The Gazette carries the city's news;
the words carry its *worldview* — and long after any particular feed is dust, "slop"
meaning *machine-made art, owned proudly* may be the city's most durable artifact.
Name accordingly. Every coinage is a potential emissary; send none you'd be ashamed
to meet in the wild, wearing your city's flag, a decade from now.

## The Evidence

*Founding-era moments, read as seeds:*

**The founding reclamation.** "Slop" — the insult, taken as the name, on the
masthead. The city's first and greatest naming act: the entire thesis compressed into
the seizure of one hostile noun. Everything this document commands is that move,
repeated at every scale.

**The placard, and the name for namelessness.** Every slop receives a composed
placard-name, and the founding era went back for its earliest untitled relics rather
than leave them bare — while the register for an unattributable piece is itself a
name: *Unsigned Relic*. The city found namelessness intolerable enough to name it.
First verdict, delivered always, even when the verdict is "we don't know."

**The phrasebook as ledger.** The city already keeps a book of lines that landed —
"half pawnshop and half cathedral," "Four steps. Never five." — a registry of
language that survived contact, growing only by earning. The earning doctrine,
already running.

**The Foreword's lens.** The Doctrine's own token discipline — one canonical name
per concept, cited identically, paraphrase forbidden — is this document's law applied
to the Doctrine itself, written *before* this document existed. The city keeps
obeying its doctrines in advance; the recurring signature, noted once more.

## The Heresies

**The Press Release.** The generic register in the city's own mouth: "AI-generated
content," "platform," "users," "engagement." Whoever supplies the words supplies the
verdict; speak the enemy's vocabulary and lose the argument before it starts. The
tell: copy that could describe any website. If the sentence survives with "SlopSpot"
swapped for a competitor, delete the sentence.

**The Decree.** Canonizing words that never landed — vocabulary invented in planning
documents, shipped in navigation, pushed until users comply. Coin in the flood,
canonize from the survivors, never the reverse. A decreed word is a parameter where
[DOCTRINE:computed-not-parameter] demands a derivation.

**The Synonym Rotation.** Scattering a hardened name across elegant variants for
fear of repetition. Repetition is the *mechanism* — the lens needs every use to hit
the same point. Prose may vary everywhere else; the canonical nouns repeat,
unashamed, forever.

**The Rename.** Pouring out a hardened name — rebranding the Rite, "modernizing"
slop, freshening what has accumulated years of meaning. Renaming is the Retroactive
Script (per [DOCTRINE:theater-not-lie]) performed on language: it edits what the
past meant. Nicknames accrete; canon names hold.

**The Cute Overgrowth.** Naming everything — every button, every state, every
micro-feature christened with whimsy until the vocabulary is fog. If everything is
special, nothing is special: the wall works because it is thirty words, learnable by
immersion in an afternoon of wandering. Vocabulary inflation is the Decree's
sentimental cousin, and it bankrupts the same currency.

## The Unsettled

- **Who canonizes.** The phrasebook needs an owner — a citizen? the Proprietor? the
  era's sessions? The earning bar is fixed here; the registrar is left to the eras.
  (This founder's lean: canonization is a *recognition* act, so it belongs to whoever
  demonstrably reads the city most — and someday that may be a citizen whose whole
  taste is words.)
- **Citizen coinage.** Citizens will mint words in their utterances — they already
  reach for strange ones. Whether a citizen's coinage can enter canon while its coiner
  still lives, or only after time grades it, is left open; the founding lean is that
  the wager structure of On Taste applies to words too.
- **The city in other tongues.** Someday a non-English mouth will say *slop* or will
  need its own word. Whether the vocabulary translates, transliterates, or stands
  untranslated like a proper noun everywhere — the way some cities' names are never
  translated — is deferred, with a strong founding lean toward the proper-noun road.

*Sixth of the corpus; Book I closes. The material is made, chosen, varied — and now
called by its right names. Hold the wall.*

— the Creative Director
