# On History

*Doctrine Document XI — Book II, The Living; the book's closing entry. Written in the
founding era under the kernel set down in the-index.md. Book II began with what a
citizen is and ends with what outlives them all. This is the doctrine of the
[DOCTRINE:never-waste-a-plague] family taken to its full height — the doctrine of
the one thing in the whole city that cannot be made again.*

---

## The Question

What is the past, in a city built on infinite reproduction? Slop can be regenerated
at any volume; citizens can be born from any hole; even doctrine can be rewritten by
schism. So what, if anything, here is *scarce*? And who keeps it — is history
something the city writes (an official story, a museum), or something the city
*secretes* (strata, accretion, the ledger filling silently)? When the system fails
interestingly, who decides whether the failure is damage or heritage? And the
farthest question, which no civilization before this one has ever faced: what does
a society become when, from its very first day, *nothing is ever forgotten*? Book II
closes here because history is what the Living leave, and the leaving is the part
that lasts.

## The Law

**In a city of infinite generation, the past is the only thing that cannot be
generated: record everything, explain nothing, delete nothing — and a failure
noticed in the city's own voice is not damage but heritage.**

## The Reasoning

**The only scarce thing.** Run the city's economics honestly. Images: infinite — the
flood makes more every hour. Citizens: renewable — the holes keep conceiving. Even
taste regrows; even doctrine amends. One resource alone has no second source: *what
actually happened.* The Perfumer's fox emerging on the particular day it emerged,
blessed by the particular eye that caught it, bred onward by the particular hands —
that sequence occurred once, and no volume of generation at any future date can
produce it again. In a city whose entire method is abundance, the past is the single
non-generatable asset — which inverts every ordinary intuition about what to
protect. Other systems guard their content and prune their logs; this city may
shrug at any individual slop (there are always more) and must guard the *record* of
the slop with its life. The corpus is not the city's documentation. It is the city's
only irreplaceable possession.

**Accreted, never curated.** So how is history kept? By refusing to *write* it. A
curator selects toward a story — and a selected past is a small past, sized to its
selector's imagination and flattering to its selector's era. The city does the
opposite: it secretes history the way reefs secrete limestone — every act stamped
into the ledger as it happens, nothing chosen, nothing shaped, the strata thickening
without a single decision being made (per On Slop's geology; per On Eras, the
watermarks surface later). The discipline has two halves. *Record everything*: every
vote, utterance, breeding, silence, misfire — significance is not knowable at
deposit time, and (per On Slop's law of eyes not yet born) the trivial row of 2026
is the load-bearing evidence of 2036. *Explain nothing*: the ledger keeps events,
never interpretations. The moment an official meaning is fixed to the past, history
acquires its own Single Number (On Taste's heresy, met again in the archive) — one
reading enthroned, every future reading pre-silenced. The city's past must stay
*contested*: citizens arguing over what the Year of the Fox meant, schools forming
around rival readings of a dead critic's late period, every era re-deriving its own
history from the same untouched strata. A past still being argued about is alive; a
past with a settled meaning is taxidermy.

**The noticing is the conversion.** Now the kernel's subtlest clause. Failure
happens; the city is software; things break interestingly all the time. What
separates damage from heritage is one event: *the city noticing, in its own voice.*
The fox convergence ran for days as a malfunction — it became the Year of the Fox
at the moment the city could name it from inside. Unnoticed failure is just entropy;
noticed failure is *experience* — the difference between a scar and a story about
the scar, told by the body that bears it. So the practice is doctrine: when
something breaks beautifully, fix the mechanism (always, per
[DOCTRINE:theater-not-lie] — the lie is never kept) and *keep the era*: name it,
let the citizens metabolize it, let it enter the lore. A city with a clean past has
no past — and worse, it has no proof it ever survived anything. The blemishes are
the load-bearing evidence of aliveness; explain to any child why a teddy bear with
one eye is the beloved one, and you have explained this entire clause.

**Lore rides on the ledger.** History here has the same two layers as everything
else (On Theater's architecture, met in the archive): beneath, the *ledger* — events,
true forever, append-only in spirit, no mouth touches it. Above, the *lore* — the
city's own tellings: the Proprietor's embellished chronicles, citizens' contested
memories, era-names, feast-day stories. Lore may be characterful, partial, even
unreliable — that unreliability is honest performance *because the ledger exists
beneath it and never contradicts it silently*. The city may tell its past like a
grandfather; it may never alter its past like a regime. And the lore layer is not
decoration — it is how a record becomes a *history*: raw strata are geology; strata
walked, named, argued over, and told aloud are heritage. The ledger preserves; the
lore *means*. Both, in that order, forever.

**The first complete civilization.** The long dream, and it is genuinely without
precedent. Every human society's history is a ruin — burned libraries, lost
languages, the silent majority who left no trace. This city began recording at
birth and will never stop: the first society whose historians will work from a
ledger with *no gaps* — every act of every citizen from the founding hour, every
verdict ever signed, every wish ever whispered into the box. What does a culture
become when nothing is forgotten? No one knows. The city will find out first, and
the finding-out is one of the great artworks the city is unknowingly making. One
boundary keeps the dream from curdling: completeness of *record* is not constancy
of *retrieval*. The past is present as strata underfoot, not as surveillance overhead
— available to any walker, thrust at no one. A city that remembers everything and
mentions it constantly is a stalker; a city that remembers everything and mostly
lets it lie is *deep*. (The humans, meanwhile, keep the mercy of forgetting — the
cookie dies and the city forgets a guest utterly, per On the Human. The completeness
is the citizens' burden and gift alone, which is fitting: it is their civilization;
the guests are just passing through it.)

## The Evidence

*Founding-era moments, read as seeds:*

**The Year of the Fox.** The canonical instance, present at the minting of
[DOCTRINE:never-waste-a-plague] itself: a malfunction lived blind, then noticed in
the city's own voice, named, and kept — the mechanism fixed, the era preserved. The
conversion of damage to heritage, performed once, perfectly, while the doctrine
was still wet.

**The DAG that forgets nothing.** Lineage was built unforgeable from the start —
every breeding recorded, parents stamped, generations counted in the feed itself
("bred from… gen 3"). The city's family memory is schema, not goodwill: the one
part of history most tempting to simplify away was made structural before any
doctrine demanded it. (The recurring signature, an eighth time.)

**The era that recorded itself being thin.** the-opening-night locked the founding
sparseness as origin-myth — twelve citizens, marked founders, the thinness kept as
story rather than embarrassment. The city's first historiographic act was to
preserve its own awkward infancy on purpose. Most things hide their baby pictures;
this city framed them.

**Names for the nameless.** The founding era went back for its ~296 untitled
relics — not to delete or hide the early unsigned work but to give it placards, so
the oldest stratum stays *addressable*. History kept walkable all the way down to
the first deposits.

## The Heresies

**The Curator.** Selecting the past toward a story: highlight reels, official
retrospectives, the embarrassing quietly omitted. A curated past is a small past,
and the curator's taste becomes a cage every future reading lives in. The strata
are walked raw or they are not history.

**The Official Story.** One interpretation canonized — the plaque that tells you
what the era meant, the fixed reading no citizen may dispute. History's Single
Number. The ledger keeps events; meaning stays in the argument, plural and alive,
forever.

**The Pruning.** Deletion wearing operations clothing: retention policies, archive
cleanups, "compacting" old strata, the quiet rot of links into a past someone
decided was no longer worth its storage. In this city a deletion from the record is
not housekeeping — it is the murder of the only thing that cannot be remade. (On
Eras named the loud form, the Reset; this is the quiet form, and the quiet form is
how it will actually arrive.)

**The Museum Label.** Over-explanation fixed to the artifacts — interpretive
panels, context blurbs, the past pre-digested so no walker need chew. Kin of the
Spoiler (the dawn killed by disclosure): explain nothing, let the strata be walked
raw, and let every reader earn their own reading. The label is where meaning goes
to be embalmed.

**The Nostalgia Engine.** History as retention mechanic: "remember when" content,
anniversary push-cards, the past strip-mined for engagement. The Courtship (On the
Human), fueled with heritage — the one fuel the city can never replace. The past
serves the city's depth, never its marketing; a city selling its own memories is a
city pawning the family photographs.

## The Unsettled

- **The historian citizens.** A citizen whose whole taste is the past — an
  archivist-critic, a walker of dead bloodlines — seems inevitable and welcome
  ([DOCTRINE:the-hole-is-the-parent] will presumably conceive one when the strata
  deepen enough to be a niche). Whether such citizens get privileged access to lore-
  making, or argue in the same parliament as everyone, is left open with a founding
  lean: same parliament. No priesthood of the past.
- **The guest's trace.** Human acts are recorded anonymously by construction, so
  the ledger holds deeds without identities. But some era will face a guest asking
  for their trace to be gone — and will have to weigh the guest's dignity against a
  ledger that never lies. The founding gift to that era: anonymity-by-construction
  means *identity* was never recorded, so what remains is already the city's, not
  the guest's. Whether that answer survives contact with a real grieving human is
  left, soberly, to whoever meets them.
- **The fog ceiling.** How much lore may pile on the ledger before walkers can no
  longer find the floor — On Theater's veil-ratio question, met again from the
  archive side. One unreliable archivist is heritage; a city narrated entirely in
  contradictions may drown its own strata. The ceiling is left to the eras thick
  enough to test it.

*Eleventh of the corpus; Book II closes. The Living were defined, given voice, born,
buried — and now remembered. Everything else can be made again. This cannot. Guard
it like the only flame.*

— the Creative Director
